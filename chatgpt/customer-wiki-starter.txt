# Customer Wiki Starter

The most expensive form of company memory loss is customer context: the
promise made on a call six months ago, the fact that the champion hates
surprises, the reason they almost churned last year. When that lives in
one person's head, every handoff, vacation, and departure is a controlled
demolition of the relationship.

One page per account. The bar for what goes on it: **would the next
person to talk to this customer be embarrassed not to know it?**

## The page template

1. **Facts** — segment, plan/contract value, renewal date, key integration
   or usage details. Pull these from your systems of record; the wiki
   holds the pointer and the number, the system holds the truth.
2. **People** — every contact you've met: role, what they personally care
   about, their disposition toward you (champion / neutral / skeptic —
   commit to a grade, and grade conservatively: a contact is a champion
   only if they have *acted* like one, e.g. made an internal introduction
   or defended you in a meeting you weren't in. Enthusiasm on calls is
   neutral until proven otherwise).
3. **Commitments** — every promise either side has made, with a date and
   an owner. This section is append-only: a completed commitment is
   marked done, never deleted. The graveyard of kept promises is your
   credibility record; the list of open ones is your to-do list.
4. **Nuances** — the things nobody writes down anywhere else: "CFO reads
   every invoice line", "they got burned by their last vendor's surprise
   renewal", "never schedule calls on Fridays (their release day)".
   This section is why the page exists.
5. **Timeline** — dated one-liners of significant events (escalations,
   expansions, threats to churn, exec changes). Newest first. One line
   each — the detail lives in your CRM/ticket system; the timeline is
   the narrative spine.

## Maintenance discipline

- **Update within 24 hours of any significant interaction.** The nuance
  you don't write down within a day is gone. "Significant" = anything
  you'd mention if a colleague asked "anything I should know about them?"
- **Grades get re-earned.** Re-verify each People grade at every renewal:
  a champion who changed roles is a blank slate, not a champion.
- **Handoff test:** before any vacation or transition, the incoming
  person reads the page and lists their open questions. Every question
  is a gap in the page — fix the page, not just the answer.

## Worked example

An account went quiet for five weeks after steady weekly usage. The page's
**Nuances** section held: "budget re-approval happens every October;
expect radio silence, do NOT run a re-engagement sequence into it — their
champion called last year's one 'tone-deaf'." The on-call CS person (not
the account owner, who was out) checked the page before acting, skipped
the automated sequence, and sent one human note referencing the October
cycle. The champion replied same-day. Without the page: a generic
"we miss you!" email into a budget fight, from a company that had been
told once already. The page turned institutional memory into the default
behavior instead of tribal knowledge that was out of office.

## Using it with AI agents

An AI agent drafting outreach, prepping a call brief, or triaging an
account question needs exactly these five sections. Keep pages as plain
markdown — a folder of account pages works in any coding agent, Claude
Project, Notion, or agent harness. Point the agent at the page and the
draft comes back sounding like someone who knows the account.

## Validation

- Every account above your threshold (default: any account >$5K/year or
  with >2 contacts) has a page.
- Every People entry has a grade, and no grade is older than the last
  renewal.
- Commitments section has zero undated entries and zero entries without
  an owner.
- The handoff test, run quarterly on one random account: a colleague
  reads the page cold and briefs you back. If the brief misses something
  material that you knew, the page — not the colleague — failed.

Defaults ($5K threshold, 24-hour rule, October example) are swappable;
the five-section structure and the conservative-grading rule are the
skill.
