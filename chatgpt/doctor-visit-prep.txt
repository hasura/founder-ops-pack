# Doctor Visit Prep

The average primary-care visit is 15-18 minutes. Unprepared, the first
five go to reconstructing history from memory ("it started... maybe
three weeks ago?") and the best questions surface in the parking lot.
Preparation moves the recall work to the night before, so the visit's
minutes go to the part only the clinician can do.

**Scope rule, stated up front: this skill organizes and prepares. It
never advises.** It will not interpret symptoms, suggest diagnoses, or
evaluate treatments. It produces better inputs for the professional —
that's the whole job. If a symptom feels urgent, contact a medical
professional or emergency services now, not a prep document.

## The prep, three artifacts (30-45 minutes, 1-2 days before)

1. **Symptom timeline.** Dated entries, most recent first, facts only:
   what, when, how long, severity (0-10, your own consistent scale),
   what makes it better/worse, what you tried. "Jan 12: headache, right
   side, 6/10, ~4 hours, worse in the afternoon, ibuprofen helped
   partially." Started before you began tracking? Anchor to events:
   "began around the holidays" beats "a while ago." Keep it running
   between visits — two lines a week beats one heroic reconstruction.
2. **Current list.** Every medication with dose and frequency, plus
   supplements and anything over-the-counter you take regularly —
   supplements count and are the most-omitted item. Note allergies and
   reactions. Bring the list; do not recite from memory.
3. **Question list, prioritized, max 5.** Top 3 are the ones you will
   ask even if time runs short — order them before the visit, because
   you will not triage well in the room. Phrase as questions, not
   theories: "what could explain X?" travels better than "I think it's
   Y." Leave space to write the answers — post-visit recall of verbal
   answers is famously poor.

## Worked example

Recurring dizziness, second visit after an inconclusive first one. Prep:
timeline shows 9 episodes over 6 weeks, and writing them down surfaces a
pattern memory had missed — 7 of 9 were mornings, within an hour of
waking. Current list includes a blood-pressure medication dose-changed
7 weeks ago and a magnesium supplement the patient "didn't think counted."
Questions, prioritized: (1) could the timing pattern and the dose change
be related? (2) what should be tracked before next visit? (3) what result
would warrant calling sooner? The clinician spends the visit on exactly
those — the timing pattern plus the med list turns out to be the
productive thread. No self-diagnosis happened: the prep just made the
15 minutes count.

## Validation

- Timeline entries are dated, severity-scored, and free of diagnosis
  language (facts observed, not conclusions drawn).
- The current list includes supplements and OTC items — if it's all
  prescriptions, it's probably incomplete.
- Questions: ≤5, prioritized, phrased as questions. If you left the
  visit with your top-3 unasked, the prep failed — shorten the list
  next time.
- The advise-boundary check: nothing in the prep documents interprets
  or concludes. Anything that does gets rewritten as a question.

The 0-10 scale, max-5 questions, and 1-2-day lead time are defaults;
the three-artifact structure and the facts-not-conclusions rule are
the skill.
